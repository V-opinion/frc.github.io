Sammanfattning på svenska

Den här artikeln presenterar ett förslag om ett Multi-Religion Election System och en Faith Representatives Chamber (FRC), Trosrepresentanternas kammare. Syftet är att demokratisera relationen mellan religion och politik, så att alla trossamfund behandlas mer jämlikt – istället för att en historiskt dominerande kyrka eller religion har en särskild, ofta dold, maktställning. Modellen innebär fria trosval (Multi-Religion Elections), en gemensam kammare för alla religioner och en ny finansieringsmodell (Multi-Religion Support) som ersätter gamla, kyrkocentrerade bidragssystem.

Multi-Religion Election System: How a Faith Representatives Chamber Could Reform Democracy

The problem: An incomplete separation between state, church and politics

In many countries, especially in Europe, the separation between state and church – and more broadly between religion and politics – is incomplete. This creates several structural problems:

  • Certain Christian traditions are structurally favored over other faiths.
  • Religious influence on foreign policy and climate policy is often hidden rather than transparent.
  • Voters from different religions (or no religion) do not have equal opportunities to be represented in religiously connected political decisions.

In the European Union, Christian Democratic parties and church-linked structures have shaped key policies on issues such as climate, war, and support for different regions and conflicts. At the same time, Europe is home to Muslims, Buddhists, Hindus, Jews, and many other faiths – all with EU citizenship and voting rights, but without equivalent religious representation or influence.

This imbalance is reinforced by church elections in some countries, where certain groups have extra formal power, while believers in other traditions have no similar mechanism.

Why this matters for climate, war and democracy

When one religious tradition has disproportionate institutional influence, it can affect:

  • Climate policy and environmental regulations, including the pace of fossil fuel phase-out.
  • Decisions about war, peace and arms trade.
  • Positions on international conflicts, such as the Israel–Palestine conflict.

If religious and political power are blurred, decisions about war funding, arms exports or sanctions can be shaped by narrow religious-political alliances instead of broad democratic deliberation.

At the same time, industries such as arms manufacturing and fossil fuels profit from instability and weak environmental protection. This can make it even harder to achieve climate goals or sustainable peace, especially when moral or religious arguments are used to defend these industries or their political allies.

The core idea: Multi-Religion Election System

To address these structural problems, I propose a Multi-Religion Election System. The guiding principle is simple:

Democracy is what has given all religious communities freedom of religion. The same democratic principle should be introduced inside all religions, without exception.

In this model:

  • Every recognized faith community participates in a shared democratic framework.
  • Representation is based on transparent elections, not historical privilege or informal influence.
  • No single religion is given a permanent structural advantage in public life.

The goal is not to abolish religion in politics, but to democratize religious representation so that all faiths – including non-believers – are treated fairly.

The Faith Representatives Chamber (FRC)

At the heart of the Multi-Religion Election System is the Faith Representatives Chamber (FRC) – in Swedish, Trosrepresentanternas kammare.

What is the Faith Representatives Chamber?

The FRC is:

  • The highest representative body for all participating faith communities.
  • Completely independent from political parties and traditional parliamentary structures.
  • Elected through Multi-Religion Elections, where each faith community chooses its own representatives within a common democratic framework.

The FRC replaces existing church-specific institutions such as a General Synod that only represents a single religion. Instead of one church holding a privileged national position, all faiths are represented in a common chamber.

What does the FRC do?

The exact powers of the FRC can vary by country, but core functions could include:

  • Representing faith communities in dialogue with the state on ethical, social and cultural issues.
  • Providing input on legislation that affects religious freedom, education, social services and moral questions.
  • Acting as a check against the fusion of one single church with the state or with a specific political party.

The FRC does not replace parliaments or secular democratic institutions. Instead, it creates a democratic, pluralistic interface between religion and the state.

Multi-Religion Elections: How they work

Multi-Religion Elections are the practical application of the Multi-Religion Election System.

Key features:

  • Countries that already have church elections (for a national church) transition from church-only elections to multi-religion elections, where all authorized faith communities can elect representatives.
  • Countries without church elections – such as the USA, UK, China, India, Iran, Israel and others – can introduce new multi-religion elections to establish their own Faith Representatives Chamber.
  • Each faith community organizes its internal candidate selection according to democratic principles, while respecting fundamental rights and national law.

In this framework, no faith has automatic dominance based on tradition alone. Representation follows actual participation and democratic votes.

Multi-Religion Support: A new financial model

Alongside the electoral reform, the system also includes a new financial model: Multi-Religion Support.

This model:

  • Replaces old systems like the SST Foundation (or similar religious funding structures) and party support that are tied to specific churches or parties.
  • Distributes financial support fairly across faith communities, based on transparent criteria, not historical privilege.
  • Is designed to function independently of tax revenues, so that religious funding does not become a hidden channel for political clientelism.

The aim is to ensure that all faith communities, large and small, can participate meaningfully in public life without being dependent on a single party, state church or private lobby.

Why this reform is urgent

An unreformed system, where one religion is structurally favored and closely linked to political power, has several long-term risks:

  • Democratic deficit: Citizens of other faiths – or no faith – feel excluded and less represented.
  • Polarization: Conflicts between religious and secular groups intensify instead of being managed democratically.
  • Foreign policy bias: Certain international conflicts, such as Israel–Palestine, are approached from one dominant religious perspective, making lasting solutions harder.
  • Climate and peace undermined: Arms and fossil industries can exploit religious narratives to legitimize war, occupation or environmental destruction.

By introducing a Multi-Religion Election System and a Faith Representatives Chamber, states can:

  • Make the relationship between religion and politics more transparent and democratic.
  • Give all faith communities – and non-believers – equal structural dignity.
  • Reduce the risk that one religious tradition monopolizes moral authority in national and international politics.

Conclusion: Faith, democracy and shared responsibility

The Multi-Religion Election System and the Faith Representatives Chamber (FRC) are proposals to move from a historically Christian-dominated model toward a genuinely pluralistic and democratic relationship between religion and the state.

Instead of letting an incomplete separation between state and church be exploited for narrow political and economic interests, this model:

  • Recognizes that faith communities are important democratic actors.
  • Ensures that all faiths are represented fairly.
  • Supports peace, climate responsibility and human rights by making religious influence transparent and accountable.

In a world facing complex and controversial issues – from climate change to war and displacement – we need institutions that reflect our real religious diversity, not just our past. The Multi-Religion Election System and the Faith Representatives Chamber are intended as one such institutional answer.
